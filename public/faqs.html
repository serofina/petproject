FAQ

1What vaccinations are required?

Please bring or submit proof your dog is current with Distemper/Parvo(Sometimes called DHPP or DAPP), and Rabies.

2 Do you accept unfixed dogs?

We only accept un-nuetered or un-spayed dogs up to six months of age. All dogs older must be spayed or nuetered.

3Should I bring my dog's food?

We recommend you feed your dog at home. Most dog's are to excited to eat here. If your dog is boarding it is best you bring their food so we don't change their existing diet. If you perferr we can supply your dog with our food for $3/meal. If you provide your own food there will be no surcharge.

4 What is social Boarding?

This provides the best experience for the dogs. Dog's will spend the night with their friends in a large open area. Each night beds and blankets are placed in the play are and the lights are turned off. We offer private areas for dogs that need some alone time. We try to let them socially board as it helps ease the anxiety of the spending nights away from their family.

5 Are there any breed restrictions?

Our only breed restriction are wolves and wolf hybrids.

7 How do you keep dogs from fighting?

As social creatures, most dogs do not want to injure or harm each other unless they feel threatened. However, we screen those occasional dogs with a personality type that falls to the far end of the dog sociability spectrum and can exhibit aggressive behavior. We do this by introducing them to one of our resident staff dogs or regular client dogs designated as a temperament tester, in a safe and controlled manner. This way we can examine each dog’s behavior and reactions for aggression around other dogs. We define aggression as “the desire or intent to do physical damage to another dog or a human.” Therefore, we count all interactions between the dogs that are accepted into our daycare as communication. Just because you see some teeth or hear a growl does not necessarily mean someone is being aggressive, it’s just how dogs communicate with each other. However, just like any social creature including humans, misunderstandings, overreactions, or mistakes can happen during those communications. It’s more common to have scuffles for the younger dogs due to their social skills and emotional maturity still developing, but it can happen to anyone. It’s an unavoidable part of the joy of socializing!
